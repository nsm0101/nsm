Case 1 · PEM

Biliary Sludge

"The teenager with sickle cell and RUQ pain after dinner"

Case 1 · PEM — Biliary Sludge

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 15-year-old female with HbSS sickle cell disease, on hydroxyurea
  • 3-week history of intermittent RUQ pain, now constant for 12 hours
  • Pain worse 30–60 min after meals, especially fried foods
  • Nausea, 2 episodes of non-bilious emesis
  • No fever, no jaundice noted by family
  • Last pain crisis 4 months ago — pain feels "different"
  • Family hx: mother had cholecystectomy at 28

Exam

  • HR 102, BP 118/72, T 37.4°C, SpO₂ 98%
  • Mild scleral icterus
  • RUQ tenderness, voluntary guarding, positive Murphy's sign
  • No rebound, no peritoneal signs
  • Labs: WBC 11.2, T.bili 2.6, ALT 48, AST 52, lipase normal, retic 8%

Why Peds with Biliary Disease?

Hemolytic anemias (SCD, spherocytosis, thalassemia) · TPN dependence · Cystic fibrosis · Obesity / rapid weight loss · Prolonged fasting · Ceftriaxone pseudolithiasis (reversible) · Pregnancy

Why POCUS?

Right-upper-quadrant POCUS is bedside, fast, and rules in/out the most common biliary pathology. Test of choice for symptomatic cholelithiasis in children.

90–95%
Sens (stones)
96–98%
Specificity

POCUS Murphy's sign + GB findings outperforms either alone for cholecystitis.

Differential Dx

  • Symptomatic cholelithiasis / biliary colic
  • Acute cholecystitis don't miss
  • Choledocholithiasis / cholangitis
  • Pancreatitis (gallstone-induced)
  • Splenic sequestration / hepatic crisis (SCD)
  • FHCS / hepatitis

🚨 Sludge ≠ Innocent

Sludge can cause acute cholecystitis, gallstone pancreatitis, and biliary obstruction — especially in hemolytic disease and TPN. Treat the symptomatic patient, not the picture.

Case 1 · PEM — Biliary Sludge

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz (adolescents) · High-frequency linear for small children/infants

Depth 10–14 cm · Focus on near field · Patient ideally NPO 6h (gallbladder distended)

Technique — "7s, sweep, scan"

1

Patient supine; have them take a deep breath and hold — pushes liver/GB caudal under ribs

2

Probe at subxiphoid R, marker to patient's head; sweep laterally toward MAL

3

Identify the "exclamation point": GB long-axis + main lobar fissure pointing to portal triad

4

Rotate 90° → transverse / short axis. Sweep entire GB neck → fundus

5

Measure anterior wall perpendicular to lumen (avoid posterior wall — acoustic enhancement falsely thins it)

6

Measure CBD just anterior to portal vein; identify on "Mickey Mouse" view

7

Reposition patient L lateral decubitus if GB collapsed or sludge unclear — sludge SHIFTS, polyps don't

Diagnostic Criteria

>3
mm
Anterior wall (abnormal)
>4
mm
CBD (peds >1y)
<2
mm
CBD infants

CBD = 1 mm per decade of life (rough peds rule). Wall >3 mm + Murphy's + pericholecystic fluid = cholecystitis.

🌫️
Sludge
Low-level, non-shadowing echogenic material that layers dependently and SHIFTS with patient repositioning. "Like sand in honey."
🪨
Stones (WES sign)
Wall-Echo-Shadow: hyperechoic curvilinear stone + clean posterior shadow. Gravity-dependent, mobile.
🐭
Mickey Mouse Sign
Portal triad cross-section: portal vein (face), CBD (right ear, anterior), hepatic artery (left ear). Use to ID + measure CBD.
👉
Sonographic Murphy
Maximum tenderness with probe pressure directly over GB. 90% sens for cholecystitis when paired with wall thickening.

Signs of Acute Cholecystitis

  • Anterior wall >3 mm (most sensitive)
  • Pericholecystic fluid (anechoic stripe outside wall)
  • GB distension >4 cm transverse
  • Sonographic Murphy's sign
  • Striated / "double-wall" appearance (edema)
  • Impacted stone in GB neck (Hartmann's pouch)
Pearl — Sludge vs Tumefactive Sludge: A non-shadowing intraluminal mass that moves with repositioning = sludge. If it doesn't move, think polyp, adenomyomatosis, or GB carcinoma (rare in peds, but think mass in chronic SCD).
Pearl — Ceftriaxone biliary pseudolithiasis: Up to 40% of kids on ceftriaxone develop sludge/stones — usually asymptomatic and reverses within weeks after discontinuation. Confirm med list before sending to surgery.
Pitfall — Edge artifact / side-lobe artifact mimicking sludge: artifact disappears with repositioning probe angle. True sludge persists and layers dependently.
Pitfall — Wall thickening is NOT specific: hepatitis, CHF, hypoalbuminemia, ascites, post-prandial GB contraction all thicken the wall. Always interpret with clinical picture.

Disposition

NPO · IV fluids · analgesia (consider ketorolac if no contraindication) · CBC/CMP/lipase/GGT · If T.bili ↑ or CBD dilated → MRCP/GI consult. SCD + symptomatic gallstones/sludge → surgical consult, elective cholecystectomy (high rate of recurrent cholestasis/crises). Cholecystitis = admit, IV abx (pip/tazo or cefoxitin), gen surg.

Intussusception

"The baby that screams, then goes quiet"

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 20-month-old female, previously healthy
  • 8-hour history of episodic colicky abdominal pain — crying inconsolably, drawing legs to abdomen, then becoming lethargic/limp between episodes
  • 2 episodes of non-bilious vomiting
  • One stool described as "currant jelly" — blood and mucus mixed Late sign
  • Refuses to eat, decreased oral intake

Exam

  • HR 132, RR 28, T 38.1°C, SpO₂ 99%
  • Lethargic in mother's arms, rouses to exam
  • Abdomen: soft during quiet phase, voluntary guarding during pain; sausage-shaped mass RUQ/RLQ (50–60% cases)
  • Rectal: small amount of blood on glove

Classic Triad (only 20–40% present with all three)

Colicky pain · Vomiting · Currant jelly stool

Why POCUS?

Ultrasound is the diagnostic test of choice — replaces barium/air enema for diagnosis (not for reduction).

98%
Sensitivity
98%
Specificity

Epidemiology

  • Peak: 6 months – 3 years
  • M > F (3:1)
  • Ileocolic = 90% of cases
  • Lead point in older children (~5%)
  • Lead points: Meckel's, lymphoma, polyp

🚨 Don't Miss

Altered mental status in intussusception — can be presenting sign (opioid-like effect from distended bowel). Consider US in any lethargic toddler.

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–12 MHz · Switch to curvilinear if deeper or larger child

Color Doppler essential — assess vascularity of intussusceptum

Scanning Technique

1

Start RLQ — most common location for ileocolic intussusception

2

Sweep transversely from RLQ → RUQ → transverse colon

3

Use gentle graded compression to displace gas

4

When mass found — image in both planes (transverse + longitudinal)

5

Apply color Doppler — assess flow within intussusceptum

6

Survey for free fluid, assess for lead point

Diagnostic Criteria

>3
cm
Outer diameter (axial)
>1.4
cm
Depth of intussusceptum

Small bowel–small bowel intussusception <3 cm with no symptoms = transient, benign — no treatment needed.

🎯
Target Sign
Transverse: hyperechoic center (intussusceptum) within hypoechoic rim (intussuscipiens) — "bull's eye"
🥩
Pseudokidney Sign
Longitudinal: layered bowel wall appears like a kidney. Hyperechoic mesentery sandwiched between walls.
🌀
Crescent Sign
Hyperechoic crescentic mesenteric fat trapped within the intussuscipiens
🌈
Color Doppler
Absent flow in intussusceptum = bowel ischemia → poorer reduction success, higher OR risk
Pearl: Absent Doppler flow → warn surgery before air enema. Doesn't contraindicate reduction but signals higher failure/perforation risk.
Pearl: Free fluid alone does not predict perforation. A small amount is common. Large complex fluid = more concerning.
Pitfall: Mesenteric lymph nodes can mimic target sign. Nodes have a hyperechoic hilum and are oval, not round. Always image in both planes.
Pitfall: Failure to visualize ≠ absence. Scan the full colon. Get radiology if inconclusive and clinical suspicion high.

Disposition

NPO · IV access · Surgical consult · Air enema reduction (~80% success, fluoroscopic or US-guided) · Admit post-reduction (10–15% recurrence within 24h) · Peritonitis / failed reduction / no Doppler flow → OR

Case 3 · PEM
03

Pneumothorax

"The tall, thin teenager who can't catch a breath"

Case 3 · PEM — Pneumothorax

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 17-year-old male, tall (6'3"), thin habitus, marfanoid features
  • Sudden onset left-sided pleuritic chest pain + dyspnea while sitting in class
  • Pain sharp, worse with deep inspiration, no radiation
  • No trauma, no recent illness
  • Active smoker (vape), no IV drug use
  • No prior episodes; uncle had "collapsed lung" twice in college

Exam

  • HR 112, RR 24, BP 122/74, SpO₂ 93% RA → 97% on 2L NC, T 36.9°C
  • Mild distress, splinting on left
  • Decreased breath sounds left apex/anterior, hyperresonant percussion
  • Trachea midline · No JVD · Subcutaneous emphysema absent
  • No chest wall tenderness, no crepitus

Categories of Pediatric PTX

Primary spontaneous — tall thin adolescent males, apical blebs (this patient) · Secondary spontaneous — CF, asthma exacerbation, PJP, LCH · Traumatic — blunt/penetrating · Iatrogenic — central line, transbronchial bx, mechanical ventilation, NIPPV · Catamenial — endometriosis (rare in teens)

Why POCUS over CXR?

86–98%
LUS Sensitivity
97–99%
LUS Specificity
~50%
Supine CXR Sens

Supine CXR misses anterior PTX. LUS detects even small anterior PTX in seconds. No radiation.

🚨 Tension PTX = Clinical Dx

Hypotension + tracheal deviation + absent breath sounds + JVD = needle decompress, do not delay for imaging. POCUS is for stable patients or to confirm tube placement after.

Differential Dx

  • Pleurisy / viral pleurodynia
  • PE (in older teens, OCP, smoking)
  • Pneumonia · MSK / costochondritis
  • Pericarditis / myocarditis
  • Esophageal rupture (vomiting hx)
Case 3 · PEM — Pneumothorax

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 5–12 MHz (best for pleura) · Phased array acceptable if linear unavailable

Turn OFF harmonics, multibeam, spatial compounding — they SMOOTH artifacts. Single focal zone at pleural line. Depth 4–6 cm.

Technique — Supine PTX Scan

1

Patient supine — air rises to the most anterior point. Start at 2nd–3rd ICS, midclavicular line bilaterally

2

Probe marker cephalad (sagittal). Identify two ribs with shadow + bat sign (pleural line between rib heads)

3

Watch pleural line for 5+ respiratory cycles — sliding = visceral on parietal pleura shimmer

4

If no sliding: switch to M-mode across pleural line — seashore (normal) vs barcode/stratosphere (PTX)

5

Slide laterally toward axilla searching for the lung point — pathognomonic for PTX

6

Scan multiple zones bilaterally — anterior, lateral, post-axillary. Posterior basal for hemothorax/effusion if trauma

Three-Step Diagnostic Algorithm

  • Step 1: Absent lung sliding (SENS only; can be false +)
  • Step 2: Absent B-lines (B-lines = no PTX, 100% specificity if seen)
  • Step 3: Lung point identified (100% SPECIFIC for PTX)

All three together → diagnostic. Lung point absent in complete (large) PTX — collapsed lung never touches chest wall.

🦇
Bat Sign
Two rib shadows ("wings") with pleural line spanning between ("body"). Confirms you're at pleura before assessing sliding.
🏖️
Seashore vs Barcode
M-mode: seashore (waves above, sand below) = normal sliding. Barcode/stratosphere (parallel lines throughout) = absent sliding = PTX.
📍
Lung Point
Transition zone where sliding lung returns into a sliding-free field with each inspiration. 100% specific. Marks size/extent of PTX.
⚡
B-lines
Vertical hyperechoic artifacts from pleural line to screen bottom. Presence of any B-line at that spot = visceral pleura present = NO PTX there.
Pearl — Find the lung point: Start anterior (in PTX region), then slowly slide lateral/inferior. The moment sliding reappears with each inspiration is the lung point. Quantifies PTX size: anterior lung point = small; mid-axillary = large.
Pearl — "Lung pulse": If you see cardiac-pulsation transmitted to the pleural line WITHOUT sliding, lung is still apposed (atelectasis, mainstem intubation, apnea) — NOT pneumothorax. Useful to exclude PTX after intubation.
Pitfall — Things that mimic absent sliding: mainstem intubation, COPD bullae, ARDS, severe asthma with hyperinflation, pleural adhesions, subcutaneous emphysema (will also abolish sliding AND prevent imaging the pleura — clue: comet-tail artifacts in soft tissue).
Pitfall — Pleural line above rib level: if you don't ID the bat sign first, you may be measuring chest wall fascia and falsely call PTX. Confirm 2 ribs + pleural line between them.

Disposition

O₂ 100% NRB (Henry's law accelerates resorption) · Small primary spontaneous PTX (<2 cm at apex) stable patient: observation + repeat imaging 4–6h. Large or symptomatic PTX: needle aspiration or small-bore pigtail (12–14F) at 4th–5th ICS mid-axillary (triangle of safety). Admit · Pediatric surgery / pulm consult · CT for blebs if recurrent · Counsel on flight/diving restrictions.

Cardiac POCUS

"The post-viral teenager with chest pain — myocarditis or pericarditis?"

Case 4 · PEM — Cardiac

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 12-year-old male, previously healthy
  • 5 days of fever, cough, myalgias — improved 2 days ago
  • Now: substernal chest pain, worse supine, better leaning forward
  • Progressive fatigue, decreased exercise tolerance, "feels short of breath walking upstairs"
  • 1 episode of pre-syncope this morning
  • Sister had recent viral URI; no COVID exposure reported

Exam

  • HR 128, BP 96/58 (low for age), RR 24, SpO₂ 96%, T 38.0°C
  • Mildly ill-appearing, JVP elevated, capillary refill 3s
  • Heart: distant heart sounds, soft S3 gallop, no friction rub today (was present yesterday at PCP)
  • Lungs: scattered bibasilar crackles
  • Liver edge palpable 2 cm below costal margin
  • ECG: diffuse low-voltage QRS, electrical alternans, PR depression
  • Troponin: 0.48 ng/mL (elevated), BNP 820

The Clinical Question(s)

(1) Is there pericardial effusion? · (2) Is there tamponade physiology? · (3) Is LV function depressed (myocarditis)? · (4) Does the IVC suggest fluid responsiveness or congestion?

5 Quick Questions of Cardiac POCUS

  • 1. Is there an effusion?
  • 2. Is there tamponade physiology?
  • 3. Is the LV global function normal/reduced?
  • 4. Is the RV dilated (strain)?
  • 5. What's the IVC doing (volume status)?

Goal-directed echo (not formal echo) — answer binary questions in 2–3 min.

🚨 Tamponade is CLINICAL + ECHO

Beck's triad (hypotension + muffled sounds + JVD), pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg, AND echo findings (RA/RV diastolic collapse, IVC plethora). Never make pericardiocentesis decision on echo alone.

Differential

  • Viral myocarditis (coxsackie, adeno, COVID, parvo)
  • Pericarditis ± effusion
  • MIS-C (kids, post-COVID 2–6 wks)
  • Acute rheumatic carditis
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy
  • Anomalous coronary artery (sports/syncope clue)
Case 4 · PEM — Cardiac

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Phased array 2–5 MHz (cardiac preset) · Linear acceptable for very small infants

Indicator orientation: cardiac convention (indicator right of screen). Most US machines auto-flip when "Cardiac" preset is selected. Depth: 12–16 cm in teens.

Four Core Views

1

Parasternal Long Axis (PSLA)

L sternal border 3rd–4th ICS, marker R shoulder. See LV, LA, AV, MV. Look for posterior effusion, LV size, EPSS >7 mm = poor EF.

2

Parasternal Short Axis (PSSA)

Rotate 90° clockwise. "Donut" of LV with mitral valve at top. Assess LV function (eyeballed FAC), septal motion, regional WMA, "D-sign" RV strain.

3

Apical 4-Chamber (A4C)

Apex (PMI), marker patient L. All 4 chambers visible. Compare chamber sizes (RV should be ≤⅔ LV). Look for chamber collapse and TR.

4

Subxiphoid (Sub4 + IVC)

Probe just below xiphoid, aimed L shoulder, near liver. Best for effusion + pericardium. Then rotate sagittal → IVC longitudinal entering RA.

Eyeballing LV Function

>55%
Normal EF
30–55%
Moderately ↓
<30%
Severely ↓

Eyeball: vigorous endocardial excursion + walls thickening >30% → normal. EPSS (E-point septal separation in PSLA) >7 mm correlates with EF <50%.

⚫
Pericardial Effusion
Anechoic stripe surrounding heart in pericardial sac. Small <1 cm, moderate 1–2 cm, large >2 cm in diastole. Distinguishes from pleural by passing anterior to descending aorta in PSLA.
💔
RV Diastolic Collapse
RV free wall buckles inward during diastole (mitral valve OPEN) — earliest sign of tamponade physiology. Use M-mode through RV to confirm timing.
↔️
RA Systolic Collapse
RA wall inversion lasting >⅓ of cardiac cycle — sensitive (94%) but earlier/less specific than RV collapse.
🫧
IVC Plethora
Dilated IVC >2 cm (or age-adjusted) with <50% inspiratory collapse — supports tamponade or RV failure. Empty/collapsing IVC ≠ tamponade.

Effusion vs Fat Pad vs Pleural Effusion

  • Pericardial fat: anterior only, hypoechoic but echogenic specks, moves with heart, does NOT extend posteriorly
  • Pericardial effusion: circumferential, anechoic, passes BETWEEN heart and descending aorta in PSLA
  • Pleural effusion: passes POSTERIOR to descending aorta in PSLA — different space entirely
Pearl — Myocarditis on POCUS: Look for globally reduced LV function, sometimes with regional WMA mimicking ischemia. Effusion is common but small. Combine with troponin + ECG to support diagnosis. Cardiac MRI is the gold standard.
Pearl — "RUSH" check in undifferentiated shock: Pump (LV/RV function, effusion) · Tank (IVC + abdominal FAST + lungs for B-lines) · Pipes (aorta for AAA — yes even in kids with connective tissue disease, DVT for PE).
Pitfall — Anterior epicardial fat misread as effusion: if "fluid" is anterior only and doesn't move dependent, it's fat. Always check posterior PSLA and subxiphoid views.
Pitfall — A small pericardial effusion does not equal small clinical impact: rapidly accumulating effusions (200 mL) can tamponade; chronic effusions of 1 L may not. Physiology over volume.

Disposition

NPO · IV access (avoid aggressive fluid in pure tamponade) · Cardiology consult immediately · Pediatric ICU if hemodynamically compromised. Pericardiocentesis only if tamponade with hemodynamic instability — ideally in cath lab; bedside US-guided subxiphoid approach if peri-arrest. Treat myocarditis: supportive, inotropic support if needed, IVIG/steroids per cardiology in MIS-C.

Femur Fracture + Nerve Block

"Diagnose the cortex, then block the nerve"

Case 5 · MSK / Proc — Femur Fracture + FICB

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 5-year-old male, fell ~6 ft from playground monkey bars 2 hours ago
  • Landed on R hip / lateral thigh; immediate inability to bear weight
  • Crying, points to mid-R thigh as area of worst pain
  • No LOC, no head injury, no other complaints
  • NPO since lunch (3 hours ago)
  • No bleeding disorder, no allergies, no prior surgeries

Exam

  • HR 130 (in pain), BP 102/64, RR 22, T 37.0°C
  • R thigh: moderate swelling mid-shaft, no obvious deformity, no break in skin
  • R lower extremity neurovascularly intact — sensation, motion, pulses normal distal
  • Pain 9/10 with any movement, refuses to flex hip/knee
  • FAST exam negative · No other injuries · GCS 15
  • X-ray pending; "midshaft femur fracture, transverse, minimally displaced"

Two POCUS Roles in This Case

(1) Diagnostic: Identify cortical disruption when X-ray is delayed or equivocal (occult fx, ED triage). (2) Procedural: US-guided fascia iliaca compartment block (FICB) for opioid-sparing pain control.

Why FICB > Opioids in Kids

75–90%
Pain reduction
~6 hrs
Duration (ropivacaine)

Lower opioid requirement · No respiratory depression · Facilitates splinting, X-ray, transport · ACEP and AAP endorse for peds femur fx.

FICB vs Femoral Nerve Block

  • FICB blocks femoral + LFCN + obturator (3-in-1)
  • Larger spread under fascia iliaca — better coverage for mid-shaft
  • Less reliant on identifying nerve precisely
  • Safer in less-experienced hands

🚨 Don't Miss

Isolated femur fx can lose up to 20% of blood volume in thigh (peds). Reassess hemodynamics; assess for compartment syndrome (rare but exists). Bilateral femur fx in young child without major mechanism → NAT screen.

Case 5 · MSK / Proc — Femur Fracture + FICB

Ultrasound & Procedural Technique

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–15 MHz for both fracture ID and nerve block

Depth 3–5 cm for groin block · MSK preset (some machines) · Use a sterile probe cover for the block

Part 1 — POCUS Femur Fx Detection

1

Probe in long axis over the anterolateral thigh, find the smooth hyperechoic cortex of femur

2

Scan proximal to distal along entire shaft — look for cortical step-off, irregularity, or hematoma overlying disruption

3

Rotate 90° to short axis at site of suspected fx for confirmation

4

"Lipohemarthrosis sign" at adjacent joint = intra-articular extension

5

Document: image showing cortical break + measure displacement if applicable

Sens ~90%, Spec ~95% for long-bone fx in trained EM users. Useful in austere settings, occult fx, triage prioritization.

Part 2 — Fascia Iliaca Block (FICB)

1

Sterile prep groin · Sedation if needed (intranasal midazolam or ketamine)

2

Probe transverse at inguinal crease — identify femoral artery (pulsatile)

3

Lateral to artery: femoral nerve (hyperechoic honeycomb, "triangular"); deep to fascia iliaca, above iliopsoas muscle

4

Slide probe ~1–2 cm lateral to artery — over sartorius/iliacus junction (away from vessel)

5

In-plane needle approach lateral → medial, pop through fascia lata then fascia iliaca (two "pops")

6

Aspirate (negative for blood) → inject 1 mL test → see hypoechoic spread under fascia iliaca, above iliopsoas in a "lens" shape

7

Inject full dose, fanning to track spread medially toward femoral nerve. Reassess pain in 15–20 min

📏
Cortical Step-off
Sharp discontinuity in the bright cortical line — even subtle <1 mm break visible in good window. Compare with contralateral if doubt.
🩸
Subperiosteal Hematoma
Hypoechoic/anechoic crescent collection along the cortex, often the first sign before obvious step-off. Common in toddler fx ("buckle").
🔻
Femoral Nerve
Hyperechoic triangular structure, lateral to femoral artery, deep to fascia iliaca, superficial to iliopsoas. "Honeycomb" appearance.
💧
Local Anesthetic Spread
Anechoic "lens" or "fluid stripe" spreading laterally to medially under fascia iliaca. If you see superficial bulge above fascia → wrong plane.

Local Anesthetic Dosing (PEDS)

  • Ropivacaine 0.2%: 0.5 mL/kg (max 3 mg/kg = 1.5 mL/kg of 0.2%)
  • Bupivacaine 0.25%: 0.5 mL/kg (max 2 mg/kg)
  • Onset 15–30 min · Duration 4–8 hours
  • Always calculate max weight-based dose BEFORE drawing up
Pearl — "Bow tie" landmark: The transition between sartorius (superior) and iliopsoas (inferior) under fascia iliaca creates a bow-tie shape; inject just under fascia at this junction for ideal spread.
Pearl — Hydrodissection: Use 1–2 mL of normal saline first to open the fascia iliaca plane safely before injecting local anesthetic. Confirms correct plane and avoids intraneural injection.
Pitfall — LAST (Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity): CNS sx (perioral numbness, tinnitus, agitation, seizure) → cardiac (bradycardia → asystole). Lipid emulsion 20% 1.5 mL/kg bolus, ACLS, call for help. Never exceed weight-based max dose.
Pitfall — Intravascular injection: ALWAYS aspirate before and during injection. Visualize needle tip throughout. The femoral artery is a few mm medial — careful with lateral approach.

Disposition

Reassess in 15–20 min — pain ↓, allows splinting and X-ray. IV access + maintenance fluids. Ortho consult. Consider traction or hip spica for definitive management based on age + fx pattern. Document neurovascular exam pre- AND post-block. Admit for pain control / surgical fixation per ortho.

Positive FAST: Ped v. MVC

"Free fluid in the unstable child — find it fast, fix it faster"

Case 6 · Trauma — Pedestrian vs MVC

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 10-year-old male, pedestrian struck by sedan ~30 mph
  • Thrown ~15 ft, lateral impact on R side; brief LOC at scene
  • EMS report: c-spine immobilized, 2 PIVs, 250 mL NS en route
  • Trauma activation; arrived 18 min after injury
  • Bystander CPR not required, no overt external hemorrhage
  • NPO since school lunch

Primary Survey (Trauma Bay)

  • A: Patent · talking · C-collar in place
  • B: RR 32, shallow; SpO₂ 94% RA; decreased breath sounds L
  • C: HR 156, BP 88/52, narrow pulse pressure, cool extremities, cap refill 4 sec; faint distal pulses
  • D: GCS 13 (E3 V4 M6), pupils 3 mm equal reactive
  • E: "Waddell's triad" suspected — R thigh ecchymosis (femur), L flank abrasion, scalp hematoma

🚨 Pediatric Compensated Shock

Shock index pediatric-adjusted (SIPA) age 6–12: HR/SBP >1.22 = high risk. This patient = 1.77. Children compensate with tachycardia until they crash.

Why eFAST Now (Not CT)

Hemodynamically unstable child + abdominal mechanism = bedside eFAST in seconds, before transport. Positive + unstable → OR / massive transfusion, not CT.

~73%
Sens (hemoperitoneum)
~94%
Specificity
95%+
PPV if unstable

Negative eFAST in stable child does NOT rule out solid organ injury — CT remains standard for definitive evaluation.

The "Six Views" of eFAST

  • RUQ — Morison's pouch (hepatorenal)
  • LUQ — Splenorenal recess + subdiaphragm
  • Pelvis — Rectovesical / Pouch of Douglas
  • Subxiphoid — Pericardial effusion
  • R + L Chest — PTX (anterior) + hemothorax (basal)

Pediatric Specifics

Spleen + liver proportionally larger, ribs more compliant → solid organ injury without rib fracture is common. Seatbelt/handlebar sign: 10× risk of hollow viscus injury. Pelvic injuries less common but more likely to bleed catastrophically.

Probe & Settings

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz (abdomen) · Phased array for subxiphoid/cardiac · Linear 5–12 MHz for chest (PTX)

FAST/Abdominal preset · Depth 12–16 cm (adolescent), 8–12 cm (school-age) · Goal: full exam in <2 min

Systematic 6-View Approach

1

RUQ — Morison's Pouch positive in our pt

Probe coronal, mid-axillary line R, marker cephalad. Fan through hepatorenal space, subdiaphragm above liver, and inferior tip of liver. Most sensitive single view.

2

LUQ — Splenorenal/Subdiaphragm

Probe more posterior + cephalad than RUQ ("knuckles to bed"). Fluid collects subdiaphragmatic first, then splenorenal. Spleen smaller window → easy to miss.

3

Pelvis — Suprapubic

Transverse + sagittal above pubis. Free fluid posterior/superior to bladder (rectovesical pouch males / POD females). Full bladder = acoustic window.

4

Subxiphoid Cardiac

Probe flat, aim L shoulder, use liver as window. Pericardial effusion = dark stripe between heart and pericardium. Look for RV diastolic collapse.

5

Bilateral Anterior Chest — PTX

Linear probe, 2nd–3rd ICS MCL. Confirm bat sign, look for sliding/B-lines. If no sliding → M-mode, look for lung point.

6

Posterior Basal Lung — Hemothorax

Same RUQ/LUQ view, fan cephalad over diaphragm. Anechoic fluid above diaphragm with "spine sign" (spine visible above diaphragm into thorax) = hemothorax.

🩸
Free Fluid (RUQ)
Anechoic stripe in Morison's pouch between liver and kidney. Even 1 mm stripe is abnormal in trauma. In our patient: ~1 cm crescent + subdiaphragmatic fluid.
🌫️
Spine Sign
In RUQ/LUQ, the thoracic spine becomes visible above the diaphragm when fluid (hemothorax) replaces normal air. Normal lung obscures spine due to air.
❤️
Pericardial Effusion
Anechoic rim around heart on subxiphoid view. ANY effusion in penetrating trauma = OR. Blunt: assess for tamponade physiology.
📍
Lung Point
Transition between sliding and absent sliding pleura = pneumothorax. 100% specific. Mid-axillary lung point = large PTX.

This Patient's eFAST

  • RUQ: POSITIVE — 1 cm anechoic stripe in Morison's pouch + subdiaphragmatic fluid (likely hepatic laceration)
  • LUQ: POSITIVE — splenorenal free fluid (concern for splenic injury)
  • Pelvis: Trace free fluid posterior to bladder
  • Subxiphoid: No pericardial effusion
  • L Chest: Absent sliding + lung point at anterior axillary line → traumatic PTX
  • R Chest: Normal lung sliding + A-lines
Pearl — Trendelenburg trick: If you suspect free fluid but Morison's pouch looks clean, tilt the patient slightly head-down (if c-spine cleared) — fluid migrates cephalad and the stripe becomes more apparent. Even small repositioning reveals occult fluid.
Pearl — Serial scans win: A single negative FAST has limited NPV in peds. If clinical concern persists or hemodynamics worsen, REPEAT in 15–30 min — bleeding may accumulate over time. Serial FAST sens approaches 90%.
Pitfall — Perinephric fat / epicardial fat / ascites: Fat = mildly echogenic, no fluid level, no shifting. Pre-existing ascites is uncommon in healthy peds but possible (nephrotic syndrome, oncology). Always check echogenicity and look at clinical context.
Pitfall — Retroperitoneal blood is INVISIBLE on FAST. Pelvic fractures, renal injuries, duodenal hematomas may bleed posteriorly and never show as intraperitoneal fluid. Unexplained instability + negative FAST = consider retroperitoneal bleed → CT or OR.

Disposition — Our Patient (Positive FAST + Unstable)

Activate massive transfusion protocol (1:1:1) · Pediatric trauma surgery to bedside · Direct to OR for exploratory laparotomy + L chest tube · TXA per pediatric trauma protocol if <3 hr from injury · NPO · Type & cross · CT only if stabilized post-op and ongoing concern for occult injury · Anticipate hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy (lethal triad).
