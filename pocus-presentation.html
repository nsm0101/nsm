Gallbladder Sludge

"Murphy-positive teen with a sand-filled gallbladder"

Case 1 · PEM — Gallbladder Sludge

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 14-year-old female, BMI 31, presents with 3 weeks of intermittent RUQ / epigastric pain
  • Worse 30–60 min after fatty meals · radiates to right scapula · resolves over 1–3 hours
  • Last episode tonight after pizza — nausea, two episodes of non-bilious vomiting
  • No fevers, no jaundice, no dark urine or pale stools
  • PMH: hemolytic anemia (HS), no prior surgeries · No OCP use · No TPN, no ceftriaxone
  • FHx: mother had cholecystectomy at 28

Exam

  • HR 96, BP 118/72, T 37.0°C, SpO₂ 99%
  • Abdomen soft, RUQ tenderness, sonographic Murphy positive, no rebound or guarding
  • No scleral icterus, no Courvoisier sign

Labs

WBC 8.4 · Lipase 32 · AST/ALT 28/24 · T bili 0.9 (D 0.2) · ALP 142 · GGT 38 · Lactate 1.1
No transaminitis, no obstruction, no pancreatitis — pre-test probability of uncomplicated biliary colic is high.

Why POCUS?

RUQ POCUS is the first-line imaging for biliary disease at every age. Bedside, no radiation, real-time Murphy sign — performed in <5 min by trained PEM physicians.

~90%
EP-performed sens. for stones
~88%
Specificity

Sludge alone is less sensitive than stones; reconfirm with radiology if clinical picture demands.

Differential

  • Cholelithiasis & biliary colic
  • Acute cholecystitis (calculous / acalculous)
  • Choledocholithiasis · cholangitis
  • Gallstone pancreatitis
  • Hepatitis · FHCS (perihepatitis)
  • Functional dyspepsia · PUD

🚨 Sludge in a Child — Why It Matters

Sludge = supersaturated bile, microcrystals, mucin. Pediatric risk factors: hemolysis (sickle cell, HS), prolonged fasting/TPN, ceftriaxone, octreotide, rapid weight loss, CF, pregnancy. Can progress to stones, biliary colic, pancreatitis, cholecystitis.

Case 1 · PEM — Gallbladder Sludge

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz · Abdominal preset · Depth 8–14 cm

Thin teen → linear 7–12 MHz can resolve sludge layering more clearly. NPO ≥ 4 h ideal but not required.

Technique — 4 Views & Patient Positioning

1

Supine, subcostal sweep — probe tip in right subcostal margin, marker to patient's head. Slide laterally until the gallbladder appears as an anechoic pear.

2

"X-7" technique — measure 7 cm from xiphoid along right costal margin; the GB usually sits beneath. Rock the probe up under the ribs.

3

Left lateral decubitus — moves gas out of view, allows mobile stones/sludge to layer dependently.

4

Long & short axis — measure anterior GB wall in long axis perpendicular to wall. Find main lobar fissure connecting GB neck to portal vein ("exclamation point sign") to confirm it IS the GB.

5

Sonographic Murphy — press the probe directly over the visualized GB and ask "Does this hurt?" More sensitive than the manual exam (~86%).

Postprandial wall thickening is normal — re-measure NPO if equivocal.

Cholecystitis Criteria (need ≥ 2)

  • Stones or sludge in the gallbladder
  • Wall thickening > 3 mm
  • Sonographic Murphy sign
  • Pericholecystic fluid
  • GB distension > 4 cm transverse / hydropic

TRANSVERSE ⭕ Insert clip:
Transverse GB — sludge–bile interface Sharp horizontal interface that shifts with patient position confirms mobile sludge vs adherent mass.

Pearl: "Tumefactive sludge" is a sludge ball that mimics a mass. Re-image after rolling the patient — true sludge will always move with gravity. Color Doppler confirms no flow.
Pearl: In hemolytic disease (sickle cell, HS, thalassemia) sludge precedes pigment stones. Document baseline so progression is recognized at the next visit.
Pitfall: Side-lobe artifact in a contracted GB mimics sludge. Adjust focal zone, lower gain, change probe angle — true sludge persists across sweeps.
Pitfall: Wall thickening alone isn't cholecystitis — hepatitis, hypoalbuminemia, CHF and recent meal all thicken the wall. Anchor with Murphy + stones/sludge + fluid.

Disposition

Pain controlled · POCUS shows sludge without cholecystitis features · Labs reassuring → Discharge with low-fat diet, surgical follow-up for elective cholecystectomy discussion. Sickle cell or recurrent biliary colic = strong indication for cholecystectomy. Return precautions: fever, jaundice, intractable vomiting, persistent pain > 6 h, dark urine.

Case 2 · PEM
02

Intussusception

"The toddler who screams, draws up, and then goes limp"

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 18-month-old male, previously well, recent URI 1 week ago
  • 10 hours of episodic crying every 15–20 minutes — pulling knees up, then becoming lethargic and quiet between episodes
  • 3 episodes non-bilious emesis · refusing PO · decreased UOP
  • Last stool 4 h ago — caregiver reports "dark red, gooey"
  • Vaccinated, no surgical history, no HSP rash

Exam

  • HR 142, RR 28, T 37.4°C, SpO₂ 99%, BP 96/56
  • Lethargic but rouses · cap refill 3 s, mucous membranes tacky
  • Abdomen soft when calm; sausage-shaped mass RUQ; voluntary guarding during pain episodes
  • Rectal: heme-positive stool, no mass

Classic Triad — Only 20–40% Have All Three

Colicky pain · Vomiting · Currant-jelly stool
Currant jelly is a late sign — don't wait for it. Lethargy alone is enough to scan.

Why POCUS?

POCUS for intussusception is one of the highest-yield, most teachable pediatric applications — and ED-performed exams now rival radiology when operators are trained.

~85%
EP-performed sensitivity
~97%
Specificity

Trent et al. (Acad Emerg Med 2018); meta-analysis Riera 2021. Negative scan + low concern = safe observation. Negative scan + high concern = formal radiology study.

Epidemiology

  • Peak: 3 months – 3 years
  • Ileocolic ≈ 90%
  • M:F ≈ 3:1
  • Lead point in ~5% (more common >3 y: Meckel, polyp, lymphoma, HSP hematoma)
  • Post-viral lymphoid hyperplasia (Peyer patches) common trigger

🚨 The Lethargic Toddler

Altered mental status can be the only presenting feature ("lethargy-predominant intussusception"). Endogenous opioids from the trapped bowel suppress consciousness. Low threshold to scan any lethargic infant/toddler.

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–12 MHz (curvilinear if >25 kg)

Depth 5–7 cm · Abdominal preset · Color Doppler ready · Use copious warm gel — wiggly toddler = comfort & speed wins.

"Lawnmower" Scanning Technique

1

Start RLQ (cecum/ileocecal valve — most ileocolic intussusceptions live here).

2

Use graded compression — slow, firm pressure displaces gas.

3

Sweep up the ascending colon (RLQ → RUQ), then across transverse colon (RUQ → LUQ), then down descending (LUQ → LLQ).

4

Found a target? Image in two planes. Measure outer diameter.

5

Apply color Doppler to the intussusceptum — assess for preserved flow.

6

Survey for free fluid and trapped mesenteric lymph nodes (crescent of fat).

Small bowel–small bowel intussusception <2 cm, transient, no obstruction → usually self-resolves. Reassess if symptoms persist.

TRANSVERSE 🎯 Insert clip:
RUQ axial — "target / bullseye" Concentric hypo–hyper–hypo rings. Outer diameter > 3 cm.
LONG AXIS 🥩 Insert clip:
Pseudokidney sign Layered bowel mimics the renal cortex/sinus on long axis.
DOPPLER 🌈 Insert clip:
Color flow within intussusceptum Preserved flow = viable bowel; predicts higher air-enema success.
EXTRA 🌀 Insert clip:
Crescent of trapped mesenteric fat Hyperechoic crescent inside the intussuscipiens; lymph nodes often visible within.
Pearl: Always sweep all four quadrants — even small bowel intussusceptions in the LLQ can be transient and clinically silent.
Pearl: Absent color Doppler flow → call surgery before air enema. It doesn't contraindicate reduction, but it raises failure and perforation risk.
Pitfall: A psoas muscle in transverse can look like a "target" — always confirm in long axis. Lymph node clusters can also mimic — they have a hyperechoic hilum.
Pitfall: Failure to see ≠ exclusion. If clinical suspicion is high, get formal radiology US or an air enema for definitive diagnosis.

Disposition

NPO · IV access · IV fluids · Pediatric surgery + IR/radiology consult · Air contrast enema reduction (~80–90% success when caught early) · Admit post-reduction × 24 h (10–15% recurrence within 24 h) · Free air / failed reduction / peritonitis / absent flow → laparotomy.

Case 3 · PEM
03

Pneumothorax

"No sliding · No B-lines · Find the lung point"

Case 3 · PEM — Pneumothorax

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 15-year-old male, tall thin (6'2", BMI 18), brought in by EMS
  • Soccer collision — opposing player's knee into the right chest 30 minutes ago
  • Acute right-sided pleuritic chest pain and shortness of breath
  • No LOC, no neck pain, no abdominal pain
  • No prior PTX, no asthma, no smoking, no Marfan

Exam

  • HR 116, RR 24, BP 124/78, SpO₂ 94% on RA → 98% on 2 L NC
  • Right chest tender to palpation, ecchymosis 4th–6th ICS
  • Decreased breath sounds R apex, hyperresonant to percussion, trachea midline
  • No JVD, no subcutaneous emphysema, no flail segment

Risk Profile

Tall thin males = primary spontaneous PTX prototype. Add blunt trauma → traumatic PTX risk. Don't wait for the CXR.

Why POCUS?

Lung US for PTX is more sensitive than supine CXR and faster than CT — ideal for trauma bays, transport, and unstable patients.

~88%
Sensitivity (peds)
~99%
Specificity
~50%
Supine CXR sens.

Especially valuable when CXR is supine and small anterior PTX may hide.

Differential of Sudden Pleuritic Chest Pain + Hypoxia

  • Pneumothorax / hemothorax
  • Pulmonary contusion
  • Rib fracture(s) · flail chest
  • Cardiac contusion · pericardial effusion
  • PE (rare in peds, consider in adolescents on OCP)
  • Pneumomediastinum

🚨 Tension Physiology

Hypotension + tracheal deviation + distended neck veins + absent breath sounds = decompress NOW (5th ICS anterior axillary line in older child, 4th ICS in smaller). Don't wait for any imaging.

Case 3 · PEM — Pneumothorax

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Linear 7–15 MHz for sliding · Phased array if linear unavailable or for larger teen chest

Depth 4–6 cm · Focal zone at pleural line · MSK or lung preset · Turn OFF harmonics / spatial compounding for cleanest pleural line

Scanning Protocol (Supine Patient)

1

Probe marker to patient's head, perpendicular to ribs, longitudinal between two ribs.

2

Identify "bat sign": two ribs (with shadow) flanking the bright horizontal pleural line — the "body" of the bat.

3

Scan the most anterior/highest point first — air rises in a supine patient (typically 2nd–4th ICS midclavicular line).

4

Watch for lung sliding ("ants on a log"), B-lines, and comet tails. Any one of these excludes PTX at that interspace.

5

Apply M-mode across the pleural line — normal = "seashore"; PTX = "barcode / stratosphere".

6

Sweep laterally to find the lung point — pathognomonic; locates the edge of the PTX (correlates with size).

The 4 Findings

  • Lung sliding — shimmer/glitter at pleural line in real-time
  • B-lines — vertical hyperechoic rays from pleura to bottom of screen
  • Lung pulse — pleural movement transmitted from heart (rules out PTX)
  • Lung point — alternating sliding/no-sliding = edge of PTX (specific)
B-MODE 🦇 Insert clip:
Bat sign — ribs flanking pleural line Anchor every scan with the bat sign so you're certain you're at the pleura.
B-MODE 🚫 Insert clip:
Absent lung sliding (R 3rd ICS MCL) Pleural line is static — no shimmer, no comet tails. Highly suggestive of PTX.
M-MODE ▦ Insert clip:
"Barcode / stratosphere" sign All horizontal lines deep to the pleura = no motion = PTX.
LUNG POINT 🎯 Insert clip:
Alternating sliding / no-sliding Pathognomonic for PTX (~100% specific). Marks the lateral edge of the PTX.
🏖️
Seashore (Normal)
M-mode: straight lines above pleura (waves), grainy sand below (lung motion).
▦
Barcode (PTX)
M-mode: parallel horizontal lines top to bottom — no motion at all.
Pearl: One B-line or one comet tail at a given interspace rules out PTX at that interspace (B-lines need apposed pleura).
Pearl: A lung pulse (cardiac transmission at the pleura) excludes PTX even without sliding — useful in mainstem intubation, apnea, pleural adhesions.
Pitfall: Absent sliding ≠ PTX in: mainstem intubation, pleural adhesions, severe COPD/bullae, prior pleurodesis, ARDS. Always search for a lung point.
Pitfall: Subcutaneous emphysema obliterates the pleural line — switch sides, move probe to a different interspace, or accept CT.

Disposition (Traumatic PTX, Stable)

Supplemental O₂ · Pain control · Serial exams · Repeat lung US in 30–60 min · Small (< 2 cm) + asymptomatic → observation, admit · Large, expanding, or symptomatic → pigtail / small-bore chest tube (4th–5th ICS anterior axillary) · Trauma surgery consult.

Case 4 · PEM
04

Cardiac POCUS

"Pericardial effusion + tamponade physiology in a viral myocarditis"

Case 4 · PEM — Cardiac POCUS

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 7-year-old male, fevers/URI symptoms × 5 days
  • Now with fatigue, decreased PO, persistent tachycardia even when afebrile
  • Caregiver reports "breathing fast" and "looks pale and sleepy"
  • No chest pain (preverbal description), no syncope, no rash
  • No known cardiac history, no recent travel, vaccinations UTD

Exam

  • HR 168 (persistent), RR 36, T 37.9°C, BP 92/58, SpO₂ 96%
  • Cap refill 4 s · cool extremities · mottled knees
  • Heart: muffled tones, no gallop appreciated
  • Lungs: clear · Liver edge 3 cm below costal margin · JVD difficult to assess
  • Mental status: drowsy, appropriate when engaged

Labs / EKG

Troponin elevated · proBNP elevated · CRP up · EKG sinus tach with low voltage + diffuse ST changes.
"Cold shock" / compensated cardiogenic shock pattern in a previously well child = STOP and image the heart.

Why POCUS?

In undifferentiated pediatric shock, focused cardiac US answers four questions in < 5 minutes:

  • Effusion? Tamponade?
  • LV function — hyperdynamic, normal, or poor?
  • RV strain (PE physiology)?
  • Volume status (IVC respiratory variation)?

Pediatric Shock Differential

  • Septic — warm or cold
  • Cardiogenic — myocarditis, congenital, arrhythmia, ALCAPA
  • Obstructive — tamponade, tension PTX, massive PE
  • Hypovolemic — gastro, hemorrhage
  • Distributive — anaphylaxis, neurogenic

🚨 Don't Miss

Persistent tachycardia out of proportion to fever, gallop, hepatomegaly, fatigue with feeds → think myocarditis. Empiric fluids can tip the failing heart — start small (5–10 mL/kg), reassess after each bolus.

Case 4 · PEM — Cardiac POCUS

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Findings

🫀

Probe & Settings

Phased array 2–5 MHz (smaller footprint) · Cardiac preset

Cardiac preset reverses the screen — marker dot now to patient's right in subxiphoid, to patient's left in parasternal. Depth 10–14 cm for school-aged child.

The 4 Cardiac Windows (Goal < 3 min)

1

Subxiphoid 4-chamber — probe flat, indicator to patient's right (cardiac preset). Use liver as window. Best for effusion + tamponade.

2

Parasternal long axis (PSLA) — left 3rd–4th ICS, indicator to right shoulder. Assess LV function and pericardial effusion (vs pleural effusion = posterior to descending aorta).

3

Parasternal short axis (PSSA) — rotate 90° clockwise from PSLA. "Fish mouth" mitral & "donut" papillary muscle views. Best for global LV function.

4

Apical 4-chamber (A4C) — probe at PMI, indicator to patient's left. Compare RV and LV sizes (RV:LV normally < 0.6).

5

IVC subcostal — sagittal view, IVC into RA. Measure 2 cm from junction. >50% respiratory collapse = volume-responsive.

Pediatric Function Eyeball

≥40%
Normal EF (visual)
<30%
Severely reduced
>0.6
RV:LV (strain)

EPSS (E-point septal separation) > 7 mm = reduced LV function on PSLA M-mode.

Circumferential pericardial effusion Anechoic stripe surrounding all 4 chambers; measure greatest depth in diastole.
SUBXIPHOID ↓ Insert clip:
RV diastolic collapse — tamponade Free wall of RV scallops inward during diastole → physiologic tamponade.
PSLA 🐟 Insert clip:
PSLA — reduced LV function, EPSS > 10 mm Anterior MV leaflet fails to approach the septum during diastole.
IVC ⛲ Insert clip:
Plethoric IVC (<20% collapse) Supports elevated RA pressure / obstructive physiology.
⬇️
RV Collapse
In diastole = early tamponade. Most sensitive.
⬆️
RA Collapse
In late diastole — earliest finding but harder to see.
Pearl: Pericardial fat pad is anechoic but only anterior to the RV and moves with the heart. True effusion wraps circumferentially and is independent.
Pearl: Pleural vs pericardial effusion on PSLA — fluid anterior to descending aorta = pericardial; posterior = pleural.
Pitfall: "Eyeball EF" overestimates function in tachycardia and underestimates in bradycardia. Pair with EPSS or fractional shortening.
Pitfall: Don't anchor on a single window. PSLA + PSSA + subxiphoid + A4C — at least 3 of 4 before calling tamponade or severe dysfunction.

Disposition

Stat pediatric cardiology consult · Formal echo · Cautious 5–10 mL/kg crystalloid trial · Early inotropic support (epinephrine or milrinone) · Avoid lytics & large fluid boluses · PICU admission · Pericardiocentesis if tamponade physiology · Treat underlying viral myocarditis with supportive care, consider IVIG per institutional protocol.

Case 5 · Ortho
05

Pediatric Femur Fracture

"Cortical step-off · then put in a fascia iliaca block"

Case 5 · Ortho — Pediatric Femur Fracture

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 5-year-old male, fell from the top of monkey bars (~6 feet), landed on left side
  • Acute severe left thigh pain · refusing to bear weight · crying inconsolably
  • No LOC, no head injury, no neck pain
  • No prior fractures · no metabolic bone disease · vaccinations UTD
  • Mechanism matches history · no concern for non-accidental trauma

Exam

  • HR 130, RR 24, BP 102/64, SpO₂ 99% · alert, in distress
  • Left thigh swollen, mid-shaft tender, obvious shortening & external rotation
  • Skin intact (no open fracture) · 2+ DP pulse · cap refill 2 s · sensation intact
  • No abdominal tenderness, GU exam normal, no pelvic instability

Blood Loss Reminder

An isolated femur shaft fracture can hide 500–1500 mL of blood in the thigh in a child — anticipate, get IV access, type & screen.

Why POCUS?

Two distinct, high-yield uses:

  • Diagnostic — confirm fracture at bedside while awaiting XR, especially when XR is delayed (mass casualty, transport, austere environment)
  • Procedural — guide a fascia iliaca compartment block for rapid, opioid-sparing analgesia
~95%
Sens. long-bone fx (peds)
~99%
Specificity

Don't Miss

  • Open fracture (skin tenting/break)
  • Compartment syndrome (pain out of proportion, 6 Ps)
  • Neurovascular injury — sciatic, femoral, popliteal
  • Ipsilateral hip / knee injury
  • Non-accidental trauma — spiral/transverse femur fx in non-ambulatory child = NAT until proven otherwise

🚨 Pain Control First

Long bone fractures are severely painful. Don't make the child wait for radiology — early IV opioid + early fascia iliaca block dramatically reduces total opioid use and time to comfort.

Case 5 · Ortho — Pediatric Femur Fracture

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Fascia Iliaca Block

🦴

Probe & Settings

Linear high-frequency 7–15 MHz (curvilinear if adult-sized thigh) · MSK preset · Depth 3–5 cm

Lots of warm gel · gentle pressure · scan the uninjured side first for the child's comfort and your own reference image.

Diagnostic Scan — 6-View Sweep

1

Scan the contralateral femur first — establishes "normal" cortex appearance and calms the child.

2

On the injured leg, scan in both planes (long & short) at proximal, middle, distal thirds.

3

Look for cortical disruption, step-off, > 1 mm displacement, or angulation.

4

Reverberation artifact deep to a fracture is highly suggestive — a "double cortex".

5

Assess for periosteal hematoma (hypoechoic stripe over the cortex) and lipohemarthrosis at the knee (fat–fluid level in suprapatellar bursa).

6

Document any Salter-Harris injury at the physis — widened physis, displacement, or metaphyseal involvement.

Fascia Iliaca Block (FIB) — Highlights

Probe: linear in transverse orientation, inguinal crease.
Landmarks: from medial to lateral — NAVeL: femoral nerve, artery, vein, empty space, lymphatics. Fascia iliaca lies over the iliopsoas muscle.

Goal: deposit local anesthetic under fascia iliaca, lateral to femoral artery → covers femoral and lateral femoral cutaneous nerves.

Dose: 0.5 mL/kg of 0.25% bupivacaine (max 2 mg/kg). Aspirate every 5 mL. Watch for LAST signs.

LONG AXIS 📏 Insert clip:
Cortical step-off, mid-shaft femur Sharp discontinuity in the bright echogenic cortex with reverberation deep to the fracture line.
SHORT AXIS ⭕ Insert clip:
Periosteal hematoma over the cortex Hypoechoic stripe lifting the periosteum away from the bone — supports fracture even if cortex looks intact in this plane.
FIB SETUP 💉 Insert clip:
Inguinal crease transverse — NAVeL anatomy Identify FA / FV, iliopsoas, fascia iliaca, and the bow-tie sartorius marker laterally.
FIB INJECTION 💧 Insert clip:
Hydrodissection under fascia iliaca Anechoic spread expands the plane between fascia iliaca and iliopsoas — the "owl-eye" sign of a good block.
Pearl: A negative POCUS doesn't exclude fracture — always pair with X-ray. POCUS is best at shaft fractures, less reliable at metaphysis / physis.
Pearl: US-guided FIB reduces opioid requirement, shortens length of stay, and is well-tolerated by children. Pair with minimal sedation (intranasal fentanyl or PO acetaminophen).
Pitfall: Growth plates (physes) look like fractures — they are symmetric, smooth, and present bilaterally. Compare to the contralateral limb to avoid this trap.
Pitfall: Don't miss compartment syndrome — POCUS may show muscle edema but is not the diagnostic test. Trust the clinical exam & intracompartmental pressures.

Disposition

IV access · IV opioid + US-guided fascia iliaca block for analgesia · Long-leg splint or traction splint · Plain XR for definitive diagnosis · Orthopedics consult · Admit for spica casting (younger child) or flexible intramedullary nail (older child); screen for NAT in any non-ambulatory child with long-bone fracture.

Case 6 · Trauma

Positive eFAST · Ped v MVC

"Hemodynamically unstable + free fluid = OR"

Case 6 · Trauma — Positive eFAST

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 9-year-old female, restrained rear-seat passenger, T-bone collision, ~45 mph, prolonged extrication
  • Brief LOC at scene, GCS 14 on EMS arrival, GCS 13 on arrival
  • Complaints: abdominal pain and left shoulder pain (Kehr sign)
  • EMS: 2 large-bore IVs, 20 mL/kg NS en route
  • Activations: Level 1 trauma · pedi surgery, anesthesia, OR notified

Primary Survey

  • A: Patent · talking (decreased) · C-collar in place
  • B: RR 30, SpO₂ 96% RA, equal breath sounds, no flail
  • C: HR 148, BP 82/54, cold clammy, cap refill 5 s
  • D: GCS 13 (E3 V4 M6) · pupils 3 mm equal reactive · moves all extremities
  • E: Seatbelt sign across abdomen, left lower rib tenderness, no deformity

🚨 Hemodynamically Unstable

Shock index (HR/SBP) = 1.80, well above pediatric thresholds. eFAST is the first imaging test — faster than CT, drives the OR decision in real time.

Why eFAST Changes Disposition

In hemodynamically unstable pediatric trauma, positive eFAST → OR. In stable patients, eFAST is adjunctive — CT is still the standard for solid organ injury workup.

~73%
Sens. hemoperitoneum
~94%
Specificity

Holmes JF 2017 PECARN: routine FAST does not reduce CT use in stable peds trauma — value is in the unstable patient.

eFAST — 6 Views

  • RUQ — Morison's pouch (hepatorenal)
  • LUQ — Splenorenal + subphrenic
  • Pelvis — Pouch of Douglas / retrovesical
  • Subxiphoid — Pericardial
  • R chest — Pneumothorax / hemothorax
  • L chest — Pneumothorax / hemothorax

Pediatric Trauma Pearls

Children compensate longer → BP falls late, often a pre-arrest finding. Liver and spleen are proportionally larger and less protected. Solid organ injury more common than hollow viscus. Seatbelt sign = 10× risk of hollow viscus injury — even with normal eFAST.

Case 6 · Trauma — Positive eFAST

Ultrasound Technique, Images & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz (abdomen) · Phased array (subxiphoid) · Linear (chest — PTX)

Abdominal preset · Depth 12–16 cm to start. No need for harmonics — adjust gain so the diaphragm and kidney are crisp.

Systematic Sweep (Goal < 2 min)

1

RUQ — Morison's Pouch

Coronal, mid-axillary R, indicator to head. Sweep through hepatorenal space, subphrenic, hepatic tip, and around the gallbladder. Most sensitive single view.

2

LUQ — Splenorenal

"Knuckles to the bed" — probe is more posterior than you think. Fan through subphrenic (above spleen), splenorenal, and inferior pole. Fluid collects subphrenic first on the left.

3

Suprapubic Pelvis

Transverse + sagittal above the symphysis. Free fluid pools posterior/superior to the bladder. Empty bladder limits the view — fill foley before scanning if catheterized.

4

Subxiphoid — Pericardium

Cardiac preset · use liver as window. Look for any anechoic stripe and for RV diastolic collapse.

5

Bilateral Chest — PTX/Hemothorax

Linear probe at 2nd ICS MCL. Apply to RUQ/LUQ to look above the diaphragm for hemothorax (anechoic + mirror artifact loss).

RUQ 🩸 Insert clip:
Anechoic stripe in Morison's pouch Even a 5 mm stripe = ~250 mL fluid in an adult; less in a child. Highly significant.
LUQ 🌑 Insert clip:
Perisplenic + subphrenic free fluid Suspicious for splenic laceration. Look at the tip of the spleen and above the diaphragm.
PELVIS 💧 Insert clip:
Free fluid posterior to bladder Pouch of Douglas / retrovesical fluid — accumulates with dependent positioning.
CHEST · LINEAR 💨 Insert clip:
Bilateral lung sliding present (no PTX) Negative for PTX bilaterally despite seatbelt mechanism. Repeat after intubation.

Semi-Quantitative Free Fluid Score

0
No fluid
1–2
Small stripe · CT if stable
3+
Multiple spaces · OR if unstable
Pearl: Always check the tip of the spleen and tip of the liver — fluid first collects there before reaching the obvious hepatorenal interface.
Pearl: Serial eFAST every 15–30 min in any unstable trauma if not yet at CT or OR — sensitivity doubles with repeat exams.
Pitfall: Perinephric fat mimics fluid — fat is mildly echogenic, doesn't shift with position, and "hugs" the kidney with no sharp border.
Pitfall: Negative eFAST does not rule out injury. Retroperitoneal hematoma, early parenchymal injury without bleeding, mesenteric tear, hollow viscus injury can all be missed.

Decision Algorithm

Positive FAST + unstable → straight to OR for hemorrhage control · Positive FAST + stable → CT abd/pelvis with IV contrast · Negative FAST + unstable → repeat FAST, consider DPA, look for extra-abdominal hemorrhage, empiric laparotomy if persistent shock.

Summary

Key Takeaways

PEM Cases

Gallbladder sludge: Echogenic mobile material without shadow. Move the patient. Color Doppler rules out polyp/mass.

Intussusception: Target / pseudokidney signs. > 3 cm outer diameter. Doppler predicts reduction success.

Pneumothorax: Absent sliding + absent B-lines + barcode + lung point. Lung pulse rules it out.

Cardiac: 4 windows in < 3 min. RV diastolic collapse = tamponade. Eyeball EF + EPSS.

Femur Fracture

POCUS = bedside diagnosis AND procedural guidance. Cortical step-off + reverberation. Compare to contralateral leg.

eFAST

6 views, < 2 min. Positive + unstable = OR. Negative ≠ excluded.
