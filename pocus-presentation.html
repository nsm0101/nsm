Case 1 · PEM
01

Pyloric Stenosis

"The baby that keeps projectile vomiting"

Case 1 · PEM — Pyloric Stenosis

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 6-week-old male, born at 38 weeks, no prior issues
  • 3-day history of non-bilious projectile vomiting after every feed
  • Initially spitting up → now forceful ejection across the room
  • Still hungry and eager to feed immediately after vomiting ("hungry vomiter")
  • Last wet diaper 10 hours ago
  • No fever, no diarrhea
  • Father had pyloric stenosis as an infant

Exam

  • HR 148, RR 36, T 37.1°C, Weight 4.1 kg
  • Sunken fontanelle, dry mucous membranes
  • Abdomen: soft, non-distended, "olive" palpable in epigastrium 10–20% of cases
  • Visible peristaltic waves left → right (rare, pathognomonic)

Classic Labs

Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
Na↓ · K↓ · Cl↓↓ · HCO₃↑↑ · pH↑

Why POCUS?

Ultrasound is the gold standard for pyloric stenosis — no radiation, bedside, fast, and highly accurate.

97%
Sensitivity
99%
Specificity

Differential Dx

  • GERD / overfeeding
  • Malrotation / volvulus must exclude
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Overfeeding / formula intolerance
  • Antral web (rare)

🚨 Key Question

Non-bilious vs bilious? Bilious = malrotation until proven otherwise.

Case 1 · PEM — Pyloric Stenosis

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–15 MHz

Neonates: highest frequency available · Gain up slightly

Patient Positioning & Prep

1

Right lateral decubitus or supine — positions stomach over pylorus

2

Offer 10–20 mL of clear liquid or formula — fills stomach to trace pylorus

3

Find gallbladder first (right epigastrium) — pylorus lies medial to it

4

Follow gastric antrum distally to the pyloric channel

5

Obtain transverse AND longitudinal views — confirm both planes

Diagnostic Criteria

≥4
mm
Muscle Thickness (single wall)
≥17
mm
Channel Length
≥14
mm
Pyloric Diameter

Single wall thickness ≥4 mm is the most reliable criterion. Premature infants: adjust cutoffs.

🍩
Donut Sign
Transverse view — hypoechoic muscle ring surrounding echogenic mucosa and submucosa
🔱
Cervix Sign
Longitudinal view — thickened pyloric muscle flanks the echogenic channel, resembles a cervix
🎯
Target Sign
Concentric rings — inner echogenic mucosa, outer hypoechoic muscle on cross-section
🌊
Antiperistalsis
Real-time: observe gastric fluid moving retrograde toward esophagus
Pearl: The muscle appears hypoechoic (dark) — measure from outer edge to inner mucosa (not lumen). Don't include the mucosal layer.
Pearl: Watch in real-time for peristaltic waves that fail to open the pylorus — dynamic finding supporting obstruction.
Pitfall: Mistaking the antrum for the pylorus — the antrum IS compressible. Trace all the way to where the channel closes.
Pitfall: Air in the duodenum (shadowing) can obscure measurement. Change to right lateral decubitus, gentle pressure.

Disposition

NPO · IV access · 0.9% NaCl 20 mL/kg bolus → maintenance · Correct alkalosis BEFORE OR · Surgical consult for pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure) · Not a surgical emergency until electrolytes corrected

Case 2 · PEM
02

Intussusception

"The baby that screams, then goes quiet"

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 20-month-old female, previously healthy
  • 8-hour history of episodic colicky abdominal pain — crying inconsolably, drawing legs to abdomen, then becoming lethargic/limp between episodes
  • 2 episodes of non-bilious vomiting
  • One stool described as "currant jelly" — blood and mucus mixed Late sign
  • Refuses to eat, decreased oral intake

Exam

  • HR 132, RR 28, T 38.1°C, SpO₂ 99%
  • Lethargic in mother's arms, rouses to exam
  • Abdomen: soft during quiet phase, voluntary guarding during pain; sausage-shaped mass RUQ/RLQ (50–60% cases)
  • Rectal: small amount of blood on glove

Classic Triad (only 20–40% present with all three)

Colicky pain · Vomiting · Currant jelly stool

Why POCUS?

Ultrasound is the diagnostic test of choice — replaces barium/air enema for diagnosis (not for reduction).

98%
Sensitivity
98%
Specificity

Epidemiology

  • Peak: 6 months – 3 years
  • M > F (3:1)
  • Ileocolic = 90% of cases
  • Lead point in older children (~5%)
  • Lead points: Meckel's, lymphoma, polyp

🚨 Don't Miss

Altered mental status in intussusception — can be presenting sign (opioid-like effect from distended bowel). Consider US in any lethargic toddler.

Case 2 · PEM — Intussusception

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–12 MHz · Switch to curvilinear if deeper or larger child

Color Doppler essential — assess vascularity of intussusceptum

Scanning Technique

1

Start RLQ — most common location for ileocolic intussusception

2

Sweep transversely from RLQ → RUQ → transverse colon

3

Use gentle graded compression to displace gas

4

When mass found — image in both planes (transverse + longitudinal)

5

Apply color Doppler — assess flow within intussusceptum

6

Survey for free fluid, assess for lead point

Diagnostic Criteria

>3
cm
Outer diameter (axial)
>1.4
cm
Depth of intussusceptum

Small bowel–small bowel intussusception <3 cm with no symptoms = transient, benign — no treatment needed.

🎯
Target Sign
Transverse: hyperechoic center (intussusceptum) within hypoechoic rim (intussuscipiens) — "bull's eye"
🥩
Pseudokidney Sign
Longitudinal: layered bowel wall appears like a kidney. Hyperechoic mesentery sandwiched between walls.
🌀
Crescent Sign
Hyperechoic crescentic mesenteric fat trapped within the intussuscipiens
🌈
Color Doppler
Absent flow in intussusceptum = bowel ischemia → poorer reduction success, higher OR risk
Pearl: Absent Doppler flow → warn surgery before air enema. Doesn't contraindicate reduction but signals higher failure/perforation risk.
Pearl: Free fluid alone does not predict perforation. A small amount is common. Large complex fluid = more concerning.
Pitfall: Mesenteric lymph nodes can mimic target sign. Nodes have a hyperechoic hilum and are oval, not round. Always image in both planes.
Pitfall: Failure to visualize ≠ absence. Scan the full colon. Get radiology if inconclusive and clinical suspicion high.

Disposition

NPO · IV access · Surgical consult · Air enema reduction (~80% success, fluoroscopic or US-guided) · Admit post-reduction (10–15% recurrence within 24h) · Peritonitis / failed reduction / no Doppler flow → OR

03

Pediatric Appendicitis

"The great masquerader"

Case 3 · PEM — Appendicitis

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 10-year-old male
  • 36-hour history of abdominal pain — started periumbilical, now localizing to RLQ (McBurney's point)
  • Anorexia, nausea, 1 episode of vomiting
  • Low-grade fever — parents noted this morning
  • Walking hunched over, pain worsens with movement

Exam

  • T 38.7°C, HR 104, RR 20, SpO₂ 99%
  • RLQ tenderness, guarding, Rovsing's sign +, Psoas sign +
  • Rebound tenderness present
  • WBC 16,200 with 82% neutrophils, CRP 48 mg/L

Pediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS)

Anorexia (+1) · Nausea/vomiting (+1)

Migration of pain (+1) · Fever ≥38°C (+1)

Cough/hop/percussion RLQ tenderness (+2)

RLQ tenderness (+2) · WBC >10k (+1)

Polymorphonuclear neutrophilia (+1)

This patient: PAS 7/10 High Risk

Why POCUS First?

POCUS + clinical score can reduce CT utilization in pediatrics. Goal: diagnose appendicitis with US, reserve CT for non-visualization or indeterminate cases.

72–85%
Sensitivity
94–97%
Specificity

Non-visualization rate 20–35% in EDs. ALARA: "US first" algorithms reduce CT use by 40–50%.

🚨 Perforation Risk

Children have higher perforation rates (~30–40%) due to diagnostic delay. Younger children (<5y) may present atypically.

Decision Algorithm (simplified)

  • PAS ≤3 → Discharge with return precautions
  • PAS 4–6 → US first; CT if non-diagnostic
  • PAS ≥7 → US; direct OR if positive; CT if non-vis
Case 3 · PEM — Appendicitis

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 7–12 MHz · Curvilinear for obese patients or deep structures

Split screen or side-by-side color Doppler can assess hyperemia

Graded Compression Technique

1

Start at ASIS, sweep medially and inferiorly with progressive gentle compression

2

Displace overlying bowel gas laterally — compress until you see psoas muscle

3

Identify the cecum (haustra, largest gas-filled structure RLQ)

4

Follow the cecum to the appendix origin at base — tip-to-base visualization required

5

Measure outer wall to outer wall in transverse plane

6

If not found: try posterior (retrocecal), or patient left lateral decubitus

Diagnostic Criteria

≥6
mm
Outer-wall diameter
<6
mm
Normal (compressible)

6–8 mm = equivocal, consider CT/MRI. Normal appendix: compressible, blind-ending, peristalsis visible in adjacent bowel.

🎯
Target Sign
Transverse: non-compressible blind-ending tubular structure >6mm, concentric rings
🪨
Appendicolith
Hyperechoic focus with posterior shadowing — increases perforation risk, should prompt urgent surgery
✨
Fat Stranding
Hyperechoic periappendiceal fat (mesenteric fat reaction) — nonspecific but supports diagnosis
💧
Periappendiceal Fluid
Anechoic free fluid adjacent to appendix or in pelvis — look for complex/loculated fluid suggesting abscess

Perforation Signs

  • Loss of echogenic submucosal layer (wall disruption)
  • Periappendiceal abscess (complex fluid collection)
  • Phlegmon — hyperechoic mass with shadowing
  • Free intraperitoneal air (rare — posterior acoustic enhancement)
Pearl: Graded compression is both diagnostic and therapeutic — it moves bowel gas aside to reveal the appendix. Never skip it.
Pitfall: Retrocecal appendix (25% of cases) — won't compress anteriorly. Roll patient left lateral, scan posteriorly.
Pitfall: Measuring the lumen instead of outer wall → underestimates diameter. Always outer-wall to outer-wall.

Disposition

NPO · IV antibiotics if perforated (pip/tazo or amp/sulbactam) · Surgical consult · Non-perforated: OR or antibiotic-first shared decision · Perforated + abscess: IV abx → interval appendectomy

Pediatric Lung Ultrasound

"The stethoscope of the 21st century"

Case 4 · PEM — Lung Ultrasound

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 4-year-old female
  • 3 days of fever to 39.5°C and worsening cough
  • Progressive tachypnea, now working hard to breathe
  • Decreased oral intake, no sick contacts initially
  • No prior lung disease, vaccinations up to date
  • Father had URI 1 week ago

Exam

  • T 39.3°C, HR 148, RR 42, SpO₂ 91% on RA → 97% on 2L NC
  • Moderate subcostal/intercostal retractions
  • Right: decreased breath sounds at base, dullness to percussion
  • Left: clear
  • CXR would show: RLL opacity with blunting of right costophrenic angle

Clinical Question

Pneumonia vs parapneumonic effusion? Does the effusion need drainage?
Lung US can answer both questions at bedside, in real-time.

Why Lung US over CXR?

96%
LUS Sensitivity
93%
LUS Specificity
87%
CXR Sensitivity

No radiation · Bedside · Dynamic assessment · Guides procedural planning

Causes of Pediatric Effusion

  • Parapneumonic (most common)
  • Empyema (complicated)
  • Malignancy (leukemia, lymphoma)
  • Chylothorax
  • Heart failure (transudate)

🚨 Danger Signs

Septated effusion · Loculated complex fluid · Lung herniation into effusion · All = empyema until proven otherwise

Case 4 · PEM — Lung Ultrasound

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Phased array (adults, older kids) · Linear (neonates/infants — highest resolution)

No special lung preset needed. Harmonics off for B-lines. Depth 6–8 cm for most peds.

Scanning Zones (BLUE Protocol)

6 zones per side: anterior upper/lower, lateral, posterior upper/lower/basal

Ant Upper
Ant Lower
Lateral
Post Upper
Post Lower
Post Basal

Posterior basal zones most sensitive for pneumonia (gravity-dependent consolidation)

Normal Lung US Findings

  • Pleural line: bright, hyperechoic, moves with respiration
  • Lung sliding: shimmering movement of visceral on parietal pleura
  • A-lines: horizontal reverberation artifacts (air in lung = normal)
  • Seashore sign: M-mode — "waves on a beach" above pleural line, granular below

Effusion Assessment

  • Simple/anechoic: transudate or early exudate
  • Complex/echogenic: exudate, hemorrhage, empyema
  • Septations: fibrinous stranding within fluid = complicated
  • Measure largest AP dimension above diaphragm
  • Mark safe spot for thoracentesis or chest drain placement
⚡
B-lines
Vertical laser-like hyperechoic artifacts from pleural line to bottom of screen — move with sliding. ≥3 per field = pathologic. Interstitial fluid.
🫁
Consolidation
Tissue-like (hepatization) echogenicity replacing normal air-filled lung. Jagged border (shred sign) at air-consolidation interface.
💨
Air Bronchograms
Dynamic = patent airways (pneumonia). Static = atelectasis. Fluid bronchograms = anechoic tubes in consolidation = complicated.
🌊
Sinusoid Sign
M-mode: lung moves toward pleura in inspiration creating sinusoidal pattern — confirms free-flowing effusion (vs empyema)
Pearl: Dynamic air bronchograms (hyperechoic dots moving toward probe during inspiration) = pneumonia, not atelectasis. This distinction can change management (antibiotics vs physiotherapy).
Pearl: Compare bilateral posterior zones — even subtle asymmetry in B-line density suggests pathology on the side with more.
Pitfall: Skin fold or subcutaneous emphysema can abolish lung sliding — don't diagnose pneumothorax on absence of sliding alone without other features (absent B-lines + lung point).
Pitfall: Right-sided "effusion" below the diaphragm = intrahepatic fluid or ascites. Always identify the diaphragm first.

Disposition

O₂ to maintain SpO₂ ≥94% · IV amoxicillin/ampicillin (typical) or ceftriaxone · Simple effusion: treat underlying pneumonia · Complex/empyema: chest tube or VATS, surgery consult · Admit all with SpO₂ <95% or significant effusion

Case 5 · Urology

Testicular Torsion

"Time is testicle" — every hour matters

Case 5 · Urology — Testicular Torsion

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 14-year-old male
  • Woke from sleep with sudden onset left testicular pain 5 hours ago
  • Nausea, 1 episode of vomiting
  • No fever, no dysuria, no trauma
  • No prior episodes
  • Denies sexual activity

Exam

  • HR 98, afebrile, BP 118/70
  • Left testis: high-riding, horizontal (transverse) lie
  • Left testis tender to light touch, swollen, firm
  • Cremasteric reflex: absent left, present right
  • Prehn's sign negative (elevation doesn't relieve pain)
  • Scrotal skin: minimal erythema, no warmth (early presentation)

Bimodal Age Distribution

Peak 1: Perinatal (extravaginal torsion) · Peak 2: 12–18 years (intravaginal, bell-clapper deformity)

Bell-clapper deformity = bilateral in 40% → both testicles at risk

Time to Salvage

>90%
Salvage <6 hrs
50%
Salvage 12–24 hrs
<10%
Salvage >24 hrs

🚨 Critical Rule

If clinical suspicion is high for torsion, do not delay to OR for imaging. POCUS is adjunct, not a gatekeeper. When in doubt, call urology first.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Torsion of appendix testis (most common misdiagnosis)
  • Epididymo-orchitis
  • Incarcerated inguinal hernia
  • Trauma / hematocele
  • Tumor (painless, slow onset)
Case 5 · Urology — Testicular Torsion

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

High-frequency linear 12–15 MHz · Hockey stick probe for smaller patients

Highest sensitivity color/power Doppler settings · Low PRF · Color box large enough to cover full testis

Technique

1

Start with the normal side — establish baseline flow for comparison

2

Gray scale bilateral: assess size, echotexture, hydrocele, epididymis

3

Apply color Doppler bilaterally — use same settings both sides

4

Power Doppler: more sensitive for low-flow states (late torsion)

5

Image spermatic cord in transverse — look for whirlpool sign

6

Document any asymmetry in flow — even reduced flow is abnormal

Diagnostic Accuracy

88–97%
Sensitivity
97–99%
Specificity

Sensitivity lower for intermittent torsion (may have normal flow at time of scan). Clinical suspicion overrides imaging.

🔇
Absent Flow
No color Doppler signal in affected testis vs normal contralateral — the most important finding. Absent = torsion until proven otherwise.
🌀
Whirlpool Sign
Twisted spermatic cord seen on B-mode or color Doppler — "pinwheel" appearance at cord entry. Pathognomonic.
🔶
Heterogeneous Echotexture
Late finding — loss of normal homogeneous pattern indicates infarction/necrosis. Poor prognostic sign.
💧
Reactive Hydrocele
Anechoic fluid around testis — common in torsion, epididymo-orchitis. Not specific but often present.

Torsion of Appendix Testis

  • Small hyperechoic nodule near epididymal head
  • "Blue dot sign" on exam (pathognomonic if seen)
  • Normal testicular flow on Doppler
  • Treatment: NSAIDs, supportive care (self-limiting)
Pearl: Always compare bilaterally with identical Doppler settings. Asymmetric flow — even if some flow present — is abnormal and warrants urgent urology consultation.
Pearl: The whirlpool sign (twisted cord) can sometimes be seen in B-mode — always scan the cord. Its absence does not exclude torsion.
Pitfall: "Present flow" does not exclude torsion. Partial torsion (<360°) or early/intermittent torsion may have preserved flow. Treat the patient, not the image.
Pitfall: Epididymo-orchitis shows increased flow (hyperemia). If you see hyperperfusion = NOT torsion (but confirm with clinical picture and consider both diagnoses co-existing in teens — uncommon but possible).

Disposition

Urology emergency consult immediately · NPO · IV access · Analgesia · Informed consent for bilateral orchiopexy (bell-clapper repairs both sides) · If no urologist available: manual detorsion "open the book" lateral-to-medial + transfer

eFAST Exam

"Ultrasound in resuscitation is the rule, not the exception"

Case 6 · Trauma — eFAST Exam

Clinical Presentation

History

  • 16-year-old male, unrestrained rear passenger
  • High-speed MVC, T-bone collision, airbag deployed
  • EMS: GCS 13 at scene, now 14 in ED
  • Complaining of abdominal and chest pain
  • No obvious external hemorrhage
  • Pelvis stable on exam

Primary Survey (Trauma Bay)

  • A: Patent, C-collar in place
  • B: Tachypneic RR 28, SpO₂ 96%, decreased L breath sounds
  • C: HR 138, BP 88/58, cool clammy skin, cap refill 4s
  • D: GCS 14, pupils equal and reactive
  • E: Seatbelt sign across abdomen, multiple abrasions

🚨 Hemodynamically Unstable

Shock index (HR/SBP) = 1.57 — high hemorrhage risk. eFAST is the first test — faster than CT.

Why eFAST Changes Everything

In pediatric trauma, positive eFAST + hemodynamic instability = hemorrhagic shock until proven otherwise. No time for CT.

~73%
Sensitivity (hemoperitoneum)
~94%
Specificity

Negative eFAST does NOT rule out injury (solid organ injury may not free-bleed initially). Serial eFAST every 30 min if concern persists.

eFAST Views (6 total)

  • RUQ — Morrison's pouch (hepatorenal)
  • LUQ — Splenorenal
  • Pelvis — Pouch of Douglas / rectovesical
  • Subxiphoid — Pericardial
  • R chest — Pneumothorax / hemothorax
  • L chest — Pneumothorax / hemothorax

Pediatric Differences

Children compensate longer → BP drops late. Liver and spleen proportionally larger and less protected. Solid organ injury more common than hollow viscus. Seatbelt sign = 10× risk of hollow viscus injury.

Case 6 · Trauma — eFAST Exam

Ultrasound Technique & Findings

🔬

Probe & Settings

Curvilinear 2–5 MHz (abdomen) · Phased array (cardiac/subxiphoid) · Linear (chest — PTX)

No special setting. Depth: start at 12–16 cm, adjust per view.

Systematic Approach (Goal: <2 min)

1

RUQ — Morison's Pouch

Coronal view, mid-axillary line R. Look in hepatorenal space, subphrenic, around gallbladder. Most sensitive for hemoperitoneum.

2

LUQ — Splenorenal

More posterior than RUQ. Fan superior (subphrenic), inferior (splenorenal), check for free fluid. Harder due to posterior position.

3

Pelvis — Suprapubic

Transverse and longitudinal above bladder. Free fluid collects posterior/superior to bladder (POD or rectovesical pouch). Full bladder helps.

4

Subxiphoid — Cardiac

Subcostal 4-chamber. Pericardial effusion = dark stripe around heart. Tamponade physiology: RV collapse in diastole.

5

Bilateral Chest — PTX/Hemothorax

Linear probe, 2nd ICS MCL. Absent sliding + absent B-lines + lung point = PTX. Hemothorax: anechoic fluid above diaphragm, compressed lung.

🩸
Free Fluid
Anechoic (early) or echogenic (clotted) fluid in dependent spaces. Even a thin 5mm stripe = significant volume.
❤️
Tamponade
Pericardial effusion + RV collapse during diastole + IVC plethora. Physiologic tamponade = immediate pericardiocentesis.
💨
Pneumothorax
Absent lung sliding + absent B-lines + "barcode sign" on M-mode + lung point (specific). Sensitivity ~86–98%.
🔴
Hemothorax
Anechoic/hypoechoic fluid above diaphragm. Look in thoracic views and also LUQ/RUQ for subphrenic fluid.

Semi-Quantitative Free Fluid Score

0
No fluid
1–2
Small (<1 cm stripe) · Consider CT if stable
3+
Large (clot, multiple spaces) · OR if unstable
Pearl: Perinephric fat can mimic free fluid. Fat is slightly echogenic, does not shift with position change, and has no sharp border with the kidney.
Pearl: The liver is your best friend in RUQ — identify it first, then the kidney below, then look in the space between them (Morison's pouch). Even 1mm of fluid here is significant.
Pitfall: Ascites in a trauma patient — free fluid doesn't always mean hemorrhage. Look for echogenicity (blood = variable, exudate = echogenic swirling). Clinical context is everything.
Pitfall: Negative FAST in hemodynamic instability ≠ no intra-abdominal injury. Retroperitoneal hematoma, early solid organ injury, mesenteric tears won't show on FAST. Repeat and escalate.

Decision Algorithm

Positive FAST + unstable → OR (hemorrhage control) · Positive FAST + stable → CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast · Negative FAST + unstable → Repeat FAST, consider DPL, re-examine, or empiric laparotomy

Summary

Lung US: B-lines · Dynamic air bronchograms · Assess effusion character

Testicular Torsion

Always compare bilateral flow. Absent flow = OR. Don't let a "normal" POCUS delay urology consult if clinical suspicion is high.

>90%
Salvage <6h
<10%
Salvage >24h

Whirlpool sign = pathognomonic. Bell-clapper = bilateral risk.

eFAST

6 views. Goal <2 minutes. Positive + unstable = OR. Negative does NOT rule out injury.

  • RUQ most sensitive
  • LUQ is most posterior
  • Pelvis needs full bladder
